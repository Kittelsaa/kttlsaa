AI has rapidly moved from a niche tech conversation to a mainstream political issue. Politicians around the world are sounding alarms. Grassroots organizations are forming, protests against data center construction are spreading, and elected officials are now being forced to pick a side on AI.

Markets are focused on chip supply chains, energy infrastructure, and which companies will win the AI race, but are completely ignoring public sentiment. A town in the US state Wisconsin is attempting to recall its mayor for allowing an OpenAI data center to be built. Activist groups are physically blocking construction. If this scales, it directly threatens the future cash flows of Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, and others.

Today, less than half of Americans view AI favorably. Less than a third say they trust it. The internet generated enthusiasm even at the consumer level. AI is generating anxiety, higher bills, and a sense that the benefits are flowing only to billionaires. People are experimenting with AI but not waking up feeling their lives are better. The narrative has gotten completely away from the industry, consumers see nothing but higher electric bills and doomsday warnings.

Household electricity bills have risen 67% in areas near data centers. These facilities employ almost nobody locally, consume enormous energy, yet pay minimal taxes. Middle class households should not be subsidizing AI infrastructure. Data centers should at minimum be taxed to cover the incremental energy costs they impose on surrounding communities.

AI has also become culturally associated with the tech billionaire class; Musk, Sam Altman, the broader Silicon Valley ecosystem. The explosion of Epstein-related revelations has made the public increasingly hostile to that entire class of people. Public hatred of the "Epstein class" is bleeding directly into hatred of AI, and that sentiment will eventually hit markets. These two things are not separate.

There is also deep frustration with AI founders who cashed out hundreds of millions in shares and then suddenly became vocal about AI's dangers. You built this, you profited from it, now you're catastrophizing without offering any real solutions. The criticism is specifically aimed at people who held equity in companies like Anthropic and waited until after vesting to raise alarms.

### The Data Center Bubble

---

The AI infrastructure buildout is being called the largest capital deployment in the history of technology, bigger than the moon race, bigger than the atomic bomb. Goldman Sachs projects the top hyperscalers will spend $1.15 trillion on infrastructure between 2025 and 2027. For 2026 alone, aggregate capex among the big five is estimated at $600 billion. Amazon is spending more on infrastructure in a single year than the entire GDP of Morocco.

But the economics were never sound and the cracks are now visible everywhere.

Oracle is a software business in structural decline. Its licensing division regularly misses analyst estimates. It changed the way it reported revenue multiple times since 2019. It has taken on $56 billion in debt and $248 billion in upcoming lease obligations. Its only major tenant for these massive data centers is OpenAI, a company that lost at least $9 billion in 2025 and is believed to be burning far more. For OpenAI to honor its $300 billion five-year agreement with Oracle, it would need to spend more in its final contract year than the entire 2024 annual revenue of Microsoft Azure ($75 billion), implying revenues exceeding $200 billion, putting it in the same league as Meta. That is not a prediction. That is the basic math.

Blue Owl Capital pulled out of a $10 billion data center funding deal with Oracle, citing "unfavorable economics" the same phrase now appearing across multiple cancelled or shelved projects. US banks are increasingly reluctant to lend. The only institutions still willing are Asian banks; SMBC, Sumitomo, and MUFG, and even they are charging a price premium. When you start seeing any of them get cold feet, the implications are industry-wide.

Between mid-December 2025 and early 2026, 41 data centers were cancelled, shelved, or delayed. The Uptime Institute projected that as much as 50% of all announced mega data centers will never be built. That estimate is likely optimistic.

The Stargate Abilene project illustrates the problem clearly. It has 8 buildings. As of mid-December, only 96,000 out of 450,000 GPUs had been delivered and delivered does not mean installed, turned on, or in use. Two buildings out of eight exist. The project was supposed to be done by mid-2025, then end of 2025, then early 2026, now end of 2026. It will not be done then either. The project currently has only 200 megawatts of the 1.4 gigawatts it will need. A gas power plant is being built to partially address this, it will likely not be complete by end of 2026, and even if it is, it covers only a quarter of the required power.

Getting a data center connected to the power grid takes up to 8 years in some cases. Loan repayment schedules are typically 6 years. You cannot pay off a loan before you can even turn the building on.

The GPU obsolescence trap makes it worse. Data centers take years to build. GPUs must be purchased upfront. Nvidia sells on advance order only. By the time most of these data centers are operational, the GPUs inside them will be one to two generations obsolete. The next GPU generation after Blackwell, Vera Rubin will still fit current racks, but the generation after that, Kyber, will require entirely new rack infrastructure. The data centers being built today may be physically incompatible with the hardware of 2027-2028. Oracle's Blackwell GPUs are reportedly already operating at negative 100% gross margins.

The circular financing makes the whole picture absurd. Amazon has a $38 billion seven-year deal with OpenAI. Amazon gives OpenAI $50 billion, OpenAI hands it back as payment for cloud services. Microsoft gives OpenAI $9 billion, OpenAI hands it back. The same dollars moving in a circle. SoftBank, which is supposed to put in another $30 billion, had to sell all its Nvidia stock and take out a $15 billion margin loan on its ARM stock just to fund what was already owed. It does not have the assets for another $30 billion. OpenAI raised $41 billion in 2025, said in June it would only need $17 billion more by 2027, and seven months later needed another $100 billion. These are not the numbers of a company being frugal with capital.

### The Layoffs 

---

Amazon is not cutting 30,000 jobs because of too many managers or bureaucracy. They are cutting because free cash flow turned negative, specifically negative $4.8 billion in a single quarter at the exact moment capital expenditure hit $125 billion, the highest of any company on Earth. 75% of that capex is going directly to AI infrastructure. They also raised $12 billion in bonds to fund data centers. The layoffs are a capital reallocation: human headcount converted into compute capacity.

The contradiction speaks for itself. The company posted one of its strongest quarters ever revenue hit $180 billion up 13% year-over-year, AWS growing at 20%, net income surged 38%, stock jumped 10% after earnings and then announced the largest layoff in the company's 30-year history. The strength of the business makes the layoffs impossible to explain through any cultural or organizational narrative.

The math: 30,000 employees at approximately $200,000 all-in compensation each equals roughly $6 billion in annual savings. Against $125 billion in capex that sounds trivial but compared to a quarterly free cash flow that went negative, $6 billion per year is the difference between funding expansion internally or issuing more debt.

In 2024, Amazon generated $38 billion in free cash flow. By Q3 2025, quarterly FCF had gone negative. The trailing 12-month figure dropped 61% year-over-year. Free cash flow margin collapsed from 8.73% of sales to 2.7%. Capex jumped from $83 billion in 2024 to $125 billion in 2025, a 61% increase in one year. Bank of America analysis found that aggregate capex among the big five now consumes 94% of operating cash flows after dividends and buybacks.

CEO Andy Jassy frames this as a culture problem, too many layers, too much bureaucracy, Amazon needs to operate like a startup. That narrative is carefully constructed to obscure what the balance sheet makes obvious. Jassy has three audiences: employees, investors, and regulators. For employees, "too many layers" is gentler than "you cost too much." For investors, admitting financial pressure would tank the stock. For regulators, framing it as routine restructuring avoids congressional scrutiny. The tell: in June 2025, four months before the first layoffs, Jassy sent a memo explicitly warning AI would mean Amazon needs fewer people. By October he was saying on earnings calls that the layoffs were "not AI-driven."

Amazon is not alone. Microsoft cut 15,000 people. Meta's year of efficiency continues. Intel eliminated 24,000 positions. Over 1.1 million job cuts hit the economy in 2025, the highest since the pandemic. What makes this cycle different from 2001 and 2008: the companies doing the cutting are not struggling. They are some of the most profitable enterprises in human history. The shift from human capital to physical infrastructure is structural, not cyclical.

For workers who remain, Amazon managers now track AI tool usage via dashboards. Performance reviews increasingly factor in how effectively employees leverage automation. The implicit message is clear: justify your existence by being more productive than the machines, or the machines come for your role next. You are not being replaced by AI, you are being replaced by the need to buy more GPUs.

### The Grift

---

The SaaS industry entered its zombie phase around 2022. Between 2018 and 2022, 30-40% of all private equity deals were for software companies. PE firms loaded these businesses with debt, expecting to flip them for multiples. But software growth plateaued. The zero-interest-rate era after 2008 and COVID made debt cheap and flooded money into SaaS on the assumption that these companies would grow in perpetuity. The only thing that grows forever is cancer. Software hit its ceiling around 2022 and the money just hadn't caught up to that reality yet. Now hundreds of billions of dollars worth of software companies sit in PE and VC portfolios worth less than what was paid for them, unable to be sold, not really growing, just existing.

SaaS companies essentially have three options left. First, build genuinely new and useful features, which is mostly not happening. Second, raise prices without improving the product, which is "SaaSflation", Microsoft is the prime example, forcing AI add-ons into subscriptions and charging more for them, a 12% annual price increase industry-wide. Third, acquire another company and staple it onto the side to manufacture the appearance of growth, Salesforce is the expert at this, having bought Slack, Informatica, and others while claiming AI revenue that is largely just absorbed acquisition revenue.

AI was supposed to be the thing that kickstarted growth again within software. It hasn't worked because AI features are not that useful and every company has the same ones. But it has created a powerful narrative that frightened executives are buying. That is where Sam Altman and Dario Amodei come in. The pitch is simple: your growth is slowing, but spend money on AI, maybe lay some engineers off, and growth will return. None of it is true. AI increases operational costs, doesn't replace software, and the models are not generating real utility at scale. But it confirms what panicked executives want to believe. The grift works because the targets want to be conned.

Enterprise software being sold to executives who don't use it, on the advice of salespeople, based on things the executive read on LinkedIn, that is how the industry already worked. AI features are just that same dynamic applied to smaller businesses and extended to consumers. A scared executive hears AI everywhere, adds AI features to their product, charges more for it, and dumps it on customers. The software stops serving the user and serves the business model instead.

The fundamental economic problem is that AI cannot replicate the SaaS cost structure. In a SaaS company, costs scale predictably, more customers means more revenue without proportional cost increases. In an AI company, each additional user makes costs go up, not down, because of how large language models work. Anthropic reportedly spends between $8 and $135 per dollar it earns on Claude Code subscriptions. That is not a business model. That is a money-losing operation with good branding.

Sam Altman's claim that AI will eventually be sold like water or electricity is simply wrong. Tokens are not a utility. GPUs are obsolete within 3-6 years. Water infrastructure lasts decades. It reflects either a complete misunderstanding of how utilities work or a deliberate attempt to lobby governments into subsidizing endless GPU infrastructure. There is no government bailout coming.

### The Future

---

Software is not being killed by AI. It is being killed by the natural end of the hypergrowth era. AI is the media's convenient explanation because accepting the real answer, that there is no next hypergrowth wave coming, is too uncomfortable for markets, analysts, and business media to confront. Claude Code is not replacing enterprise resource planning systems. The entire AI-killed-software narrative is a comfortable lie.

Companies like Figma and Google have integrated AI sensibly into their existing offerings without existential disruption. If anything, the ability to vibe-code simple SaaS tools increases trust in established, reliable software rather than replacing it. AI won't kill software, it will pressure bad software and consolidate trust around products that actually work.

What is coming is not the end of software but a major revaluation of tech stocks, a market reckoning with the reality that infinite growth assumptions built into software company valuations were always false. The AI bubble is a symptom of this larger collapse, not an independent phenomenon. Data centers going unbuilt, debt deals underwater, banks losing hundreds of millions of dollars, that is the tangible, visible proof that causes people to dump stocks and stop signing new loans.

The suggested market position reflects this reality: go short on direct AI infrastructure companies like Nvidia and Microsoft, and go long on SaaS companies that are supposedly under threat from AI disruption, because that disruption narrative is overblown and the real risk is on the infrastructure side.

Unless the tech industry produces something genuinely useful, which based on its track record since 2018, is an open question, the reckoning is unavoidable. The era of software being sold as a fair exchange of value has ended. What follows is the correction.
