Talking about AI doesn't feel productive. Every conversation I have seems to split into two camps: those who see AI as humanity's doom and those who see it as our salvation. But AI is neither good nor evil by itself. It is just a tool, just like a hammer, a computer, or a search engine.

Think about what happened with the internet. The search engine era was chaotic and full of predictions about how it would either save or destroy us. There were winners and losers, bubbles and crashes, real harm and real innovation. And at the end of it all, we got Google, a tool so embedded in daily life that most people use it without thinking twice. AI is following a similar arc. The race is messy, the money is irrational, and the hype is deafening. But what we will likely end up with, years from now, is not a revolution that rewrites what it means to be human. We will end up with a tool, probably built into our phones, our laptops, our search bars, that most people will casually use. 

While some people debate whether AI is good or bad, other people are already using it to gain power. They use it to see patterns that others cannot see. Right now, AI is being used in ways that cause real harm to real people. Tech startups are slapping "AI" onto ordinary products for the hype, with no genuine innovation behind it. In Palestine, AI is being used by Israeli forces to target people with deadly precision, leveraging data in ways that are deeply immoral. Many companies are laying off tech professionals rather than upskilling them, exhausting an entire generation of people who built their careers in this field. Data centers powering AI from Google to xAI are causing documented environmental and health damage in Uruguay, Kenya, Ireland, Chile, and across the United States. These are not abstract concerns. These are consequences landing on specific communities who had no say in the decisions that brought those data centers to their doorstep.

But crossing our arms and refusing to engage with AI will not fix any of this. It will only guarantee that when the decisions get made, ordinary people have no voice in them.

Because AI is genuinely good at certain things. It can move through documents in minutes. It can find patterns in data that would take an individual days to spot. It can translate, summarize, and explain in ways that make complex information accessible to people who were previously locked out of it. 

What you should focus on is understanding this technology well enough to know where it is useful for you and where it is not. That means demystifying it. AI is expensive, AI needs quality data, and significant personalization to actually work well. It is not magic. It does not need to become conscious. The more frequently you use it, the more clearly you see its limitations, the gaps, the errors, the places where it confidently says something wrong. The people who understand those limitations are the people who will use AI well. The people who treat it as magic will be used by it instead.

The search engine era did not need everyone to understand how PageRank worked. But it needed enough people paying attention to hold Google accountable when it started making choices that shaped what information people could access. AI needs the same thing now, at a much larger scale, much earlier in the process. We need people from all backgrounds in this conversation, not just engineers or investors. We need indigenous voices, working people, communities who are already bearing the costs of infrastructure that was built without their consent.

The winner of the AI race will eventually become as ordinary as Google to ordinary people. What is not ordinary is the moment we are in right now, before that winner is decided.
